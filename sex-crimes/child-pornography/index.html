How California Charges These Cases: The Penal Code 311 Series

California prosecutes child pornography under a group of statutes known as the Penal Code 311 series. PC 311.11 makes it a crime to knowingly possess or control material that depicts a person under 18 engaged in or simulating sexual conduct. PC 311.1 and PC 311.2 cover sending, transporting, duplicating and distributing that material, and PC 311.4 applies to anyone accused of using or persuading a minor to participate in producing it.

The word "knowingly" is the heart of these cases. The District Attorney must prove that you knew the material was on your device and that you knew it depicted a minor. Files that arrive through automatic downloads, shared networks, or someone else's use of your computer do not satisfy that element on their own — and Marc holds the prosecution to it.

Penalties for a Child Pornography Conviction in California

The penalties for child pornography charges range from a misdemeanor to a felony.

If charged with a misdemeanor possession offense, you can face a $1,000 fine and up to 1 year in county jail.

In comparison, a felony charge can lead to a state prison sentence with a fine of up to $100,000.

Felony possession under PC 311.11 carries 16 months, two, or three years in state prison, and aggravating factors — such as a prior conviction or a large volume of images — increase that exposure and make prosecutors far less flexible in negotiations.

Whether the conviction is a misdemeanor or a felony, you will also be required to register as a sex offender under Penal Code 290. For many clients, that consequence weighs heavier than the jail exposure itself, which is why it deserves its own discussion below.

When the Case Goes Federal

Many San Diego cases begin with a task force rather than a routine police report. The San Diego Internet Crimes Against Children (ICAC) Task Force and federal agencies like Homeland Security Investigations monitor peer-to-peer networks and follow up on cyber-tips reported by internet providers. If the U.S. Attorney files charges under 18 U.S.C. §§ 2252 or 2252A, your case moves to federal court in the Southern District of California in downtown San Diego.

Federal exposure is far harsher than state exposure. A conviction for receipt or distribution carries a five-year mandatory minimum sentence, production charges carry a fifteen-year mandatory minimum, and the federal sentencing guidelines pile on enhancements for things like file counts and how the material was stored or shared. Marc defends cases in both systems — he knows how federal agents build these prosecutions and where they break down.

Sex-Offender Registration and the PC 290 Tier System

Since 2021, California no longer imposes automatic lifetime registration on everyone. Penal Code 290 now uses a three-tier system: tier one requires a minimum of ten years on the registry, tier two a minimum of twenty years, and tier three is lifetime registration. Misdemeanor possession convictions generally fall in tier one, while felony child pornography convictions can land in the highest tiers.

Registration touches everything — where you live, where you work, and how your name appears in a public database. A core part of Marc's strategy is structuring any resolution to avoid registration entirely where possible, for example by negotiating a plea to a non-registerable offense, or to keep you in the lowest tier with a clear path to petition for removal once the minimum period has run.

Defense Strategies Built on Digital Forensics

At The Law Office of Marc S. Kohnen, we believe that a mere accusation is not enough to convict a defendant. Instead, the prosecution must prove every element beyond a reasonable doubt. For that, we work on our end to ensure justice is served.

Our potential defenses to child pornography allegations include evaluating if you accessed the material by clicking a wrong link. We also assess if law enforcement used illegal means to obtain evidence.

Additionally, it is becoming common for others to conceal their own wrongdoings on your electronic devices. This can also lead to being falsely accused of possession.

Marc frequently works with independent computer forensic experts to re-examine the government's evidence. Where were the files actually stored — in a folder you used, or in a browser cache or unallocated space you never saw? Did a peer-to-peer program download and share files automatically? Was your Wi-Fi network secured, and who else had access to the device? The answers to these questions have unraveled prosecutions.

Search warrants are another pressure point. If investigators overstated their evidence to get the warrant, searched beyond its scope, or seized devices without lawful authority, Marc moves to suppress everything that flowed from the illegal search — which is often the entire case.

What to Expect in San Diego County Court

State charges are typically arraigned at the Central Courthouse in downtown San Diego, or at the branch courts in Vista, El Cajon, or Chula Vista, depending on where the alleged offense occurred. Felony cases then move through readiness conferences and a preliminary hearing, where the District Attorney's specialized sex-crimes prosecutors must show enough evidence for the case to proceed. Every stage is an opportunity to challenge the evidence, negotiate, or push for a reduction.

Marc also fights the collateral terms that make life impossible while a case is pending — bail arguments, computer and internet restrictions, and no-contact conditions — so you can keep working and supporting your family. Discretion matters enormously in these cases, and Marc handles them quietly and personally from the first call forward.
