Understanding Grand Theft Charges in San Diego

What Is Grand Theft?

Grand theft is the unlawful taking of someone else's property without their consent, with the property valued at over $950. Under California Penal Code 487, grand theft is categorized as a "wobbler" offense, meaning it can be prosecuted as either a misdemeanor or felony depending on the circumstances.

If convicted, you may face severe penalties, including substantial fines, jail time, and a permanent criminal record. Hiring an experienced San Diego grand theft defense lawyer is essential to safeguarding your future.

Types of Grand Theft

Grand theft encompasses several specific types, each with its unique legal elements:

Grand Theft by Larceny

This involves taking another person's property without permission. It requires:

  • Moving the property, even a short distance.
  • Intent to permanently deprive the owner of its value.

Grand Theft by Embezzlement

In cases of embezzlement, the property was lawfully entrusted to the defendant but was misused for personal benefit. Proving intent to deprive the owner (temporarily or permanently) is crucial for the prosecution.

Grand Theft by Trickery

This occurs when deception is used to obtain someone's property with intent to permanently deprive them of it. The prosecution must prove that the owner relied on the deception to surrender the property.

Grand Theft by False Pretense

This form involves deliberately providing false information to convince someone to hand over their property. The victim must rely on this false representation to their detriment.

Penalties for Grand Theft

The penalties for grand theft vary depending on the value of the property and the circumstances of the case:

  • Misdemeanor Grand Theft: Up to one year in county jail.
  • Felony Grand Theft: 16 months, two years, or three years in state prison.

Additional penalties may include restitution to the victim, probation, and fines.

Defense Strategies for Grand Theft Cases

At The Law Office of Marc S. Kohnen, we tailor every defense to the unique aspects of your case. Common defenses include:

Lack of Intent

We may argue that you lacked the intent to permanently deprive the property owner, undermining a critical element of the charge.

Mistaken Identity

Mistaken identity is common in theft cases. We thoroughly investigate to uncover any evidence of false accusations.

Consent

If the property owner gave you consent to take or use the property, this undermines the theft charges.

Property Value Dispute

If the value of the property is contested and falls below $950, we can aim to reduce your charges to petty theft, which carries less severe penalties.
